Leading Negotiations & Closing Deals

Why "almost done" is where most deals die

Why Deals Die at the Finish Line

You've had a great conversation. They're interested. You've showed them the product. They said yes to the next meeting. Everything looks good.

Then...

  • They don't return your email
  • They meet with your competitor
  • They say "let's revisit this in Q3"
  • They ask for a discount you can't give
  • Their boss changes their mind

The deal was never as real as you thought.

Here's what happened:

You moved into the negotiation phase without doing the hard work:

  • You didn't understand their real priorities (price? functionality? timeline? risk?)
  • You didn't know your bottom line (what can you actually offer?)
  • You didn't prepare what you'd do if they said no
  • You didn't identify where there's room to move (on both sides)

At a trade fair, deals move FAST

You might only have one conversation. You need to move from "interesting conversation" to "let's do this together" in one or two meetings. That requires strategy.

Think about a deal you've seen or been part of that fell apart. At what point did it die? Was it a real misunderstanding, or did someone not do the negotiation work?

Today, you'll learn to lead negotiations so promising conversations actually become signed agreements.

BATNA, ZOPA & Strategic Concession Language

Three concepts that separate good negotiators from great ones.

1. BATNA (Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement)

What is it: If this deal falls through, what's your next move?

You need to know your BATNA before you walk into a negotiation. Not as something you announce — but as something that grounds your confidence.

Examples:

  • "If they won't move on price, we work with their competitor instead."
  • "If they can't commit to a timeline, we focus on other markets."
  • "If they want custom features we can't afford, we walk away."
2. ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement)

What is it: The space where both sides can say yes.

You might want £100k for your software. They might budget £60k. But maybe there's a three-year agreement that gets them £70k/year (£210k total) where both sides are happy.

The zone exists when:

  • Your walk-away price is lower than their maximum budget
  • You can create value in different ways (price vs. terms vs. features vs. timeline)
  • Both sides want the relationship to work
3. Strategic Concession Language

What it is: How you move without appearing weak.

You don't just drop your price. You trade concessions. You connect movement to commitment.

The Language of Strategic Negotiation

"Before we go further, let me understand your priorities. Is it price, timeline, features, or risk that matters most?"

This is NOT asking for their demands. You're learning what THEY value. That's where you find the ZOPA.

"I'm willing to [concession] if you can [commitment]."

This says: I'm moving, but you're moving too. It's a trade, not a surrender.

"Let's look at the bigger picture here. What would a successful outcome look like for you — not just on price, but on the whole partnership?"

This expands the conversation beyond the one thing you disagree on. Opens up the ZOPA.

"What would need to change for this to work for both of us?"

Puts the problem-solving in their court. Invites collaboration.

"I think we have the basis for an agreement here. Let me confirm what I'm hearing: [summary]. Is that right?"

This moves toward commitment. You're testing if you're actually aligned before you shake hands.

You're selling a year-long consulting contract. You quoted £80k. They're saying their budget is £50k. Walk me through: (1) What's your BATNA? (2) Is there a ZOPA? (3) How do you respond to their budget without just dropping your price?

What Worked & What Didn't

Two negotiations. Same industry. Different outcomes. What's the difference?

Case Study A: The Failed Negotiation

Setup: You're selling marketing automation software. Annual license: £40k. They've shown genuine interest. You're now at the negotiation table.

Their opening: "We love the product. But our budget is £25k. Can you do it for that?"

You (mistake #1): "£25k is too low. We need at least £35k to make this work."

Them: "We can't go that high. Our CFO has already set the budget at £25k."

You (mistake #2): "Then I don't think we can work together. I'm sorry it didn't work out."

[Deal dies. You never found out if they could have stretched to £30k for a two-year deal, or if they had budget flexibility in Q2.]

What went wrong
  • You didn't ask questions. You just said no. You didn't understand their constraints or priorities.
  • You didn't explore the ZOPA. You assumed £35k was your floor. But was it? Could you do £30k on a two-year deal? Could they get budget approval for £28k?
  • You didn't make strategic concessions. You just said no. No trading, no "I'm willing to move if..."
  • You made it a fight. By giving them an ultimatum, you made them defensive. They're now in "prove your value" mode instead of "how do we make this work" mode.

Case Study B: The Successful Negotiation

Same setup: Marketing automation software. You quoted £40k. They open with: "We love the product. But our budget is £25k."

You (good move #1): "Thanks for being clear on budget. Before I respond, help me understand: is £25k a hard ceiling, or is there flexibility if the value is there? And what's more important — getting started this quarter, or waiting until you have more budget?"

Them: "Our CFO set the ceiling at £25k. But if we can start now and save time, there might be room. We're losing about £5k per month to manual processes right now."

You (good move #2): "Ah — so you're losing £5k/month. That's £60k a year in waste. Our software typically saves clients that amount. So let's look at the bigger picture. What if we structured this as: you pay £30k now, commit to year two at £25k, and we build in a success bonus — if you hit 40% time savings in year one, we waive 20% of year two? You save money, we're motivated to deliver results."

Them: "That's interesting. Let me take it to the CFO. But I like the logic."

[Deal not closed yet, but now it's real. You've found the ZOPA. You've connected to their actual problem (waste). And you've traded — they pay a bit more now, you take risk on outcomes. Both sides moved.]

What worked here
  • You asked before you said no. You learned that their budget wasn't as rigid as it seemed.
  • You found their pain. "£5k per month in waste" — now it's not about your software cost, it's about their opportunity cost.
  • You created a ZOPA. By offering a multi-year structure and a success-based bonus, you created value in ways beyond price.
  • You made strategic concessions. You moved from £40k to £30k now (£55k over two years). But they gave something too: commitment and risk-sharing.
  • You made it collaborative. "Let's look at the bigger picture" — you're problem-solving together, not fighting.

Negotiate in Real Time

You're at a trade fair. You've had two great conversations with a buyer. They're interested. Now it's time to negotiate the deal. I'll play the buyer. Multiple rounds. You need to move from "interested" to "let's do this."

The Scenario:

You: You're selling a three-month consulting engagement to help a manufacturing company improve supply chain efficiency. Your fee: £45k.

Them: They want it. But they've never paid more than £30k for consulting. And they're nervous — what if your approach doesn't work?

Goal: Close the deal (or move toward a clear next step) in 5 minutes.

What you need to do:

  • Understand their real constraint (budget? risk? timeline?)
  • Identify what they value most
  • Propose a structure where both sides move
  • Get to a clear commitment
5-minute negotiation round
5:00
I'm the buyer. I'm genuinely interested, but £45k is more than we've budgeted. I need you to either come down on price or give me a reason to believe this will work so my boss approves the extra spend. Go.

After the negotiation:

Reflect with me:

  • Where did you first move? Was it a strategic move or a panic move?
  • Did you understand their real constraint, or did you just react to the price?
  • What could you have asked that would have changed the negotiation?
  • If you gave a concession, what did you get in return?

Write Your Negotiation Summary

After a negotiation at a trade fair, you need to document what happened — and commit it to action. Here's the task.

Write a one-page memo that includes:

  1. Outcome: Did you close the deal, or move to a next step? Be clear.
  2. Their Priorities: What mattered most to them — price, timeline, features, risk mitigation, something else?
  3. What Moved: What concessions did each side make? (If you just dropped price with nothing in return, that's a concession to them, not a trade.)
  4. Your BATNA: If this deal had fallen through, what was your next move?
  5. Next Steps: Exactly who does what by when? ("They'll send a contract by Friday" not "we'll follow up sometime.")
Draft this memo now. Speak it aloud — would your boss understand exactly what happened and what comes next? Then tell me: Where did you feel most confident in that negotiation? Where did you feel most unsure?

Pro Tip:

The memo forces you to be clear about what actually happened. If you wrote "we negotiated the price down" — that's not a memo, that's a failure to do the real work. The memo should show: we understood their priorities, we found a creative solution, and we both moved.
