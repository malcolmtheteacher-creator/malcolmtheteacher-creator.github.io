The Academic Editor

A C2 Grammar Diagnostic

Your Role: Academic Editor

You are reviewing manuscripts submitted to an academic journal. Each submission contains one subtle grammatical or stylistic flaw. Your task is to identify the issue and suggest a correction.

At C2 level, these are not obvious errors. They involve subjunctive mood in formal contexts, precision with modals, agreement with Latin plurals, register awareness, and fine-grained control of English structure. This is the precision that distinguishes a master of English.

Practice Example 1: Get the Hang of It

Practice Sentence
"The data suggests that there is a strong correlation between the two variables."

What needs editing?

Correction
"The data suggest that there is a strong correlation between the two variables."
The Rule
Data is formally plural (the singular is datum, from Latin). In academic English, treat data as plural: "the data suggest," "the data show," "the data indicate." This is the formal register expected in scholarly writing.

Practice Example 2: No Edit Needed

Practice Sentence
"One cannot help but wonder whether the entire premise is flawed."

Is there an error here?

No Edit Required
This sentence is grammatically correct as written.
The Rule
The construction "cannot help but + bare infinitive" is grammatically correct in English. It means "cannot avoid" or "must inevitably." Examples: "You cannot help but admire their courage." It's a valid English pattern, not an error. Not every sentence in the diagnostic needs editing!

Sentence 1
"It is imperative that every delegate submits their report by Friday."
What needs editing?
Correction
"It is imperative that every delegate submit their report by Friday."
The Rule
After "it is imperative/essential/vital that...," use the subjunctive mood — the base form of the verb without -s, even for third person. The pattern is: "that + subject + base verb." So: "that she submit" (not "submits"), "that he arrive" (not "arrives"). This is mandatory in formal and academic English.

Sentence 2
"Had the data been collected more systematically, the conclusions may have been different."
What needs editing?
Correction
"Had the data been collected more systematically, the conclusions might have been different." (or "could have been")
The Rule
In the conditional perfect (unreal past), use "might have" or "could have," never "may have." The modal "may" cannot be used for hypothetical past events. "May have" is only for present speculation about the past ("She may have left already"). For unreal past conditions, use: "might have," "could have," "would have."

Sentence 3
"The committee recommended that the policy is revised before implementation."
What needs editing?
Correction
"The committee recommended that the policy be revised before implementation."
The Rule
After verbs of recommendation, suggestion, demand, or insistence (recommend, suggest, insist, demand, propose), use the subjunctive: that + subject + base verb. So: "that the policy be revised" (not "is revised"), "that she leave" (not "leaves"). This is a core feature of formal English.

Sentence 4
"The researchers concluded that the phenomena was consistent with existing theoretical models."
What needs editing?
Correction
"The researchers concluded that the phenomena were consistent with existing theoretical models." (Or: "the phenomenon was consistent...")
The Rule
Phenomena is plural (from Latin). The singular is phenomenon. So: "phenomena are/were," "phenomenon is/was." Other Latin pairs: data/datum, criteria/criterion, media/medium, strata/stratum. In academic English, precision with these forms is essential.

Sentence 5
"The authors set out to look into the relationship between socioeconomic status and health outcomes."
What needs editing?
Correction
"The authors set out to investigate the relationship between socioeconomic status and health outcomes." (Or: examine, explore, analyse)
The Rule
"Look into" is too informal for academic writing. Academic register demands single-word Latinate verbs over multi-word phrasal verbs. Replace with: investigate, examine, explore, analyse. This distinction marks the difference between casual and scholarly English.
Register & Style
Sentence 6
"The overwhelming majority of participants was in favour of the proposed changes."
What needs editing?
Correction
"The overwhelming majority of participants were in favour of the proposed changes."
The Rule
With "majority of + plural noun," the verb agrees with the plural noun (participants), not with "majority." So: "the majority of participants were" (not "was"), "the majority of students have" (not "has"). When "of + plural" follows a collective noun, treat the whole phrase as plural.
Subject-Verb Agreement
Sentence 7
"The team developed a new methodology, whereby allowed them to collect data more efficiently."
What needs editing?
Correction
"The team developed a new methodology that allowed them to collect data more efficiently." (Or: "which allowed")
The Rule
"Whereby" means "by which" and introduces a clause describing a process or arrangement, not a result. Correct: "A system whereby members vote online." Incorrect: "A method whereby allowed..." (the clause lacks a subject). Use "that" or "which" for the result of an action. Whereby is for mechanism; that/which is for outcome.
Relative Clauses & Connectors
Sentence 8
"In her seminal work, Thompson (2018) argues that globalisation was fundamentally reshaping labour markets."
What needs editing?
Correction
"In her seminal work, Thompson (2018) argues that globalisation is fundamentally reshaping labour markets." (Or: "has fundamentally reshaped")
The Rule
When citing a present-tense reporting verb ("argues," "claims," "suggests," "shows"), the reported content should match its current truth value. If the claim remains valid today, keep it present. Don't backshift the tense after a present reporting verb—that suggests the claim is no longer true. Use: "argues that... is" or "argues that... has been."

Sentence 9
"The study aimed to identify key risk factors, to assess their relative importance, and developing a predictive model."
What needs editing?
Correction
"The study aimed to identify key risk factors, to assess their relative importance, and to develop a predictive model."
The Rule
Items in a formal list must be parallel in structure. If the first two items use "to + infinitive," the third must also use "to + infinitive." Switching to -ing (developing) breaks the parallelism. Always maintain parallel form: "to identify..., to assess..., and to develop..." This is a hallmark of polished academic writing.

The Style Guide: Detailed Explanations

This is your deep reference. Each category appears below with the grammar rules that matter at C2 level.

Subjunctive Mood

After Imperative Expressions
Use the subjunctive after "it is imperative / essential / vital / important / necessary that..."
✓ "It is imperative that every delegate submit their report."
✗ "It is imperative that every delegate submits their report."
After Verbs of Recommendation
Use the subjunctive after "recommend, suggest, propose, demand, insist that..."
✓ "The committee recommended that the policy be revised."
✗ "The committee recommended that the policy is revised."
The Subjunctive Form
The subjunctive uses the base form of the verb: no -s for third person, no irregular past forms. "That he submit," "that she be," "that they arrive."
Base form: go, submit, arrive, be, have
Third person present: "He go" (not "goes")

Modal Verbs: Precision

"May Have" vs. "Might Have"
"May have" = present speculation about the past (something possibly happened).
"Might have" = conditional perfect (hypothetical past, what could have happened under different conditions).
✓ "She may have left already." (speculation)
✓ "Had we asked, she might have come." (unreal condition)
✗ "Had the data been collected better, the conclusions may have been different." (should be "might have")
Conditional Perfect: Use "Might," "Could," "Would"
For unreal past scenarios, only "might have," "could have," or "would have" work. Never "may have."
✓ "If I had known, I would have told you."
✓ "We could have succeeded if you had helped."
✗ "We may have succeeded if you had helped."

Nouns & Number: Latin Plurals

Common Latin Pairs
These come from Latin or Greek and retain their original plurals in formal English. Treat them accordingly.
Data / Datum — "The data are clear." (plural)
Criteria / Criterion — "These criteria are essential." (plural)
Phenomena / Phenomenon — "The phenomena were observed." (plural)
Media / Medium — "The media are reporting." (plural)
Strata / Stratum — "The strata show variation." (plural)
Academic Precision
In academic writing, treating "data" as plural (not as a mass noun) is standard and expected. Same with "criteria," "phenomena," etc.

Register & Style: Formal Language

Phrasal Verbs vs. Latinate Verbs
Academic writing favours single-word Latinate verbs over multi-word phrasal verbs. This signals scholarly register.
✗ "The authors looked into the relationship..." (informal)
✓ "The authors investigated the relationship..." (formal)

✗ "We need to work out the problem..." (informal)
✓ "We need to resolve the problem..." (formal)
Common Substitutions
Look into → investigate, examine, explore, analyse
Work out → resolve, determine, establish
Find out → discover, identify, ascertain
Put across → convey, express, communicate

Subject-Verb Agreement: Collective & Partitive

"Majority of" + Plural Noun
When "majority of," "some of," "all of," "part of" precede a plural noun, the verb agrees with the plural noun, not the collective.
✓ "The majority of participants were in favour." (plural)
✗ "The majority of participants was in favour." (incorrect)

✓ "Some of the students have finished." (plural)
✓ "All of the data are accurate." (plural)
Collective Nouns Standing Alone
When a collective noun (team, committee, group) stands alone, the verb can be singular or plural depending on whether you treat the group as one unit or as individuals.
✓ "The team is ready." (unit)
✓ "The team are divided on this." (individuals)
In formal American English, singular is standard; in British English, plural is common.

Relative Clauses & Connectors: Precision

Whereby: Process or Mechanism
"Whereby" (= "by which") introduces a relative clause describing a process, system, or arrangement. It does not introduce results or consequences.
✓ "A system whereby members vote online." (describes the system)
✗ "A method whereby allowed them..." (incorrect: missing subject, describes result)
✓ Use "that" or "which" for results: "A method that allowed them..." or "...a new methodology which allowed them..."
That vs. Which
Both can introduce restrictive relative clauses. "Which" is common after commas (non-restrictive); "that" is standard in restrictive clauses (no comma).
✓ "The methodology that we used was sound." (restrictive, no comma)
✓ "The methodology, which we used for years, was sound." (non-restrictive, comma)

Tense & Aspect: Academic Reporting

Present Reporting Verbs + Tense Backshift
When you cite a claim using a present-tense reporting verb ("argues," "claims," "shows"), keep the reported content in a tense that reflects its current truth value. Don't artificially backshift.
✗ "Thompson (2018) argues that globalisation was reshaping markets." (suggests it's not true anymore)
✓ "Thompson (2018) argues that globalisation is reshaping markets." (claim still valid)
✓ Or: "Thompson (2018) argues that globalisation has been reshaping markets." (from past to now)
Historical vs. Current Perspective
If citing a claim as historically situated, past tense is acceptable. But if the claim is still considered valid, present is more appropriate with a present reporting verb.

Sentence Structure: Parallelism

Parallel Lists
Items in a list or series must be parallel in grammatical form. If you use "to + infinitive" for the first item, use it for all items in the list.
✗ "The study aimed to identify factors, to assess importance, and developing models."
✓ "The study aimed to identify factors, to assess importance, and to develop models."

✗ "We wanted happiness, success, and to be free."
✓ "We wanted happiness, success, and freedom." (all nouns)
Parallel Structure with Coordinating Conjunctions
After "and," "or," "but," the structure that follows should match what came before. This is a hallmark of polished, academic prose.

Word Formation: Nominalisations

Standard Nominalisations
Know the correct noun form for common verbs. Not all verbs accept -tion or -ism endings equally.
✗ criticisation → ✓ criticism
✗ analysation → ✓ analysis
✗ emphasisment → ✓ emphasis
✓ creation (from "create")
✓ investigation (from "investigate")
✓ recommendation (from "recommend")
Why This Matters at C2
C2 is about precision and accuracy with the language system. Knowing which nominalisations are standard—not invented forms—is part of that mastery.
