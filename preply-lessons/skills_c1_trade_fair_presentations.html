Public Speaking & Presentations at Trade Events

How to command 200 people for 10 minutes without a second chance

You're On Stage

Scenario: You've just been invited to speak at the main conference stage of the trade fair. 200 people in the audience. 10 minutes on the clock. You'll never see most of them again.

What makes this different from booth conversations?

  • You can't read the room in real-time or adjust on the fly
  • You can't ask questions back — you own the airspace
  • People are sitting down, facing forward, with nowhere to hide
  • Every filler word, every pause that's too long, every "um" is visible
  • You have maybe 3 seconds to grab them before someone checks their phone

But here's what's different in YOUR favour:

On stage, you're the expert. You've earned this platform. People came to hear YOU, not just what you're selling. They're primed to listen.

Think back to a presentation you've seen that stuck with you. What did that speaker do? How did they open? What made you remember it?

In this lesson, you'll learn a framework — SPARK — that turns 10 minutes into impact. You'll practice opening strong, managing your pace, and closing in a way that makes people act.

The SPARK Framework

SPARK is designed for short, high-stakes presentations. Every element builds:

Problem
Approach

1. STORY (First 60 seconds)

Goal: Pull them in. Make it personal, make it real.

Start with a moment, a scene, a question that makes them curious. Not about your company — about THEIR world.

  • "Let me take you back to..." — puts them in a moment
  • "I walked into a client's warehouse and saw..." — visceral, specific
  • "What if I told you..." — creates tension
  • Avoid: "Today I want to talk about..." (generic, puts them to sleep)

2. PROBLEM (45 seconds)

Goal: Define the challenge clearly. Make them feel it.

This is where they nod and think "Yes, that's us." Use data to anchor it, but tell it like a human.

  • "The question we should all be asking is..." — frames the real issue
  • "Here's what the data tells us..." — gives evidence
  • "Most teams think it's X, but it's really Y." — reframes thinking

3. APPROACH (2 minutes)

Goal: Show them the path forward. Keep it simple — no more than three key points.

  • Use signposting language: "First... Second... Third..."
  • The rule of three sticks in memory
  • Each point should feel distinct, not repetitive
  • Example: "We do three things: we listen, we diagnose, we implement."

4. RESULTS (2 minutes)

Goal: Prove it works. Show evidence that matters to them.

  • One strong case study beats three weak statistics
  • "Here's what happened in practice..." — specific story, specific outcome
  • Numbers that mean something: "We cut their time by 60%" beats "We improved efficiency"
  • Quote them: "One of our partners said..." (real voice, real validation)

5. KICKSTART (1 minute, 30 seconds)

Goal: Move them. Give them something to do right now, or a thought to sit with.

  • "If you take away one thing today, it's this..." — memorable close
  • "The first step is..." — removes mystery
  • "Here's my ask..." — clear invitation
  • The close should feel like an opening, not an ending

Strong Openings vs. Weak Openings

The first 60 seconds determine whether people are present or absent. Here's what the difference looks like:

Weak Opening (Generic, disconnected)

"Today I want to talk about digital transformation in manufacturing. Digital transformation is important. Companies are investing in it. So let me tell you what we do."
Why this fails
  • Announcement: "Today I want to talk about..." — you're telling them you're starting, not starting.
  • Generic language: "Digital transformation," "important," "investing" — could be any presenter, any topic.
  • About YOU: "What we do" — but they haven't asked yet. They don't care until you've made them curious.
  • No story: Just labels. Minds wander.

Strong Opening (Story-driven, specific)

"I walked into a manufacturing plant in Belgium six months ago. The production manager showed me their planning board — it was literally a piece of paper on a wall with Post-its, moved by hand. I asked, 'How many times do you update this a day?' She said, 'Whenever we know something changed. Which is constantly. We're always out of sync.' That's the moment I realised: digital transformation isn't about technology. It's about ending confusion."
Why this works
  • Concrete scene: Belgium, production board, Post-its — I can see it.
  • Real dialogue: The conversation feels authentic. That's your credibility.
  • Reframes the issue: Not "digital transformation is important" but "it's about ending confusion" — that's YOUR insight.
  • Universal: Anyone managing operations recognises that feeling of things being out of sync.
  • Sets up everything: Now they want to know how you solve confusion.

Timed Presentations: 3 & 5 Minutes

Timing is not optional. It's your structural skeleton. When you know your timing, you know where to breathe, where to pause, and where you have room for a story vs. needing to compress.

Your First Presentation: 3 Minutes

This is your minimum viable presentation — the absolute essence. No nice-to-haves, only must-haves.

Story & Problem: 45 sec

Pull them in, then name the challenge.

Approach (Rule of 3): 45 sec

Three things. Quick. Signpost each one.

Results Snapshot: 45 sec

One proof. One number. One story.

Kickstart: 45 sec

"If you take away one thing..."

Self-Assessment After 3 Minutes

Once you've delivered, ask yourself:

  • Did I rush the opening, or did it breathe?
  • Could I actually identify the three points, or did they blur together?
  • Did my one proof feel real, or did it feel like a statistic?
  • Would someone remember the one thing I wanted them to?

Your Second Presentation: 5 Minutes

Now you have room. You can expand the approach to FOUR elements, add a second case study in results, breathe between sections.

Story & Problem: 1 min

More detail. Paint the scene.

Approach (Rule of 3–4): 1:30 min

More breathing room. Add an example to one point.

Results (Double Proof): 1:30 min

Two case studies, or one story + data.

Kickstart: 1 min

More time to land your ask and answer Q&A setup.

Q&A Technique After the Presentation

Bridge the question to your message: "That's a great question. Let me connect it back to what we just talked about..."

Three-step Q&A response:

  1. Answer the surface question in one sentence
  2. Give the deeper insight (your story, your data)
  3. Connect back to your main point: "Which is why..."
Record yourself delivering either the 3-minute or 5-minute version. Listen back. What surprised you about your pacing? Where did you rush? Where did you have pauses that felt right?

Build Your Presentation Outline

Now you'll create a complete SPARK outline for a presentation you might give at a real trade fair. Choose a product, service, or idea you know — it can be real or imagined.

Your Task (Write or Speak):

Create a SPARK outline using these prompts:

  1. Story: What's the scene? What moment made you understand the problem?
  2. Problem: What does the audience face? Name it specifically. Use one number if you have it.
  3. Approach: Three (or four) key things you do or recommend. Make each distinct.
  4. Results: One strong proof. A client story, a data point, or both.
  5. Kickstart: "If you take away one thing..." What is it? What's your ask?
Outline your presentation now. You can write it or just think it through aloud with me. Once you've got it, we'll practice the opening together. Then ask yourself: Does this feel like YOU? Would someone actually remember this?

What Makes Presentations Memorable?

  • Specificity: "€40,000 loss" beats "significant loss"
  • Human moments: Real dialogue, real people, real choices
  • One clear idea: Not everything — the ONE thing you want them to remember
  • Permission to feel: Surprise them, move them, challenge them
  • Your voice: Don't sound like a presenter. Sound like yourself.

What Makes Presentations Forgettable?

  • Lists: "We offer A, B, C, D, E, F..." (they remember nothing)
  • Corporate language: "Synergies," "optimise," "leverage" (everyone sounds the same)
  • No story: Just information transfer (your slides do this better than you)
  • Too much detail: They need essence, not exhaustion
  • Ending with a question: "Any questions?" leaves them hanging. Sit with the silence.
