The Language of Argumentation

To argue effectively about a book's importance, you need precise language. Click each expression to reveal its meaning.

be attributed to To say that something was caused by or is the result of something else. "The rise of environmentalism can be attributed to Silent Spring."
pave the way for To create the conditions that make it possible for something to happen. "Wollstonecraft's work paved the way for the suffragette movement."
call into question To cause doubt about something previously accepted. "Darwin's theory called into question the idea of divine creation."
undermine To gradually weaken or damage something (an idea, a system, authority). "The Communist Manifesto sought to undermine the foundations of capitalism."
give rise to To cause something to exist or happen. "Orwell's 1984 gave rise to concepts like 'Big Brother' and 'Orwellian'."
stand the test of time To continue to be valued or relevant over a long period. "Aesop's Fables have stood the test of time for over 2,500 years."
bring about To cause something to happen; to make something occur. "Uncle Tom's Cabin helped bring about a shift in public opinion on slavery."
have far-reaching consequences To have effects that extend widely or over a long time. "The publication of The Rights of Man had far-reaching consequences for democracy."

Advanced Emphasis: Inversions & Cleft Sentences

In Part 1 you used "If it hadn't been for..." to talk about imaginary consequences. Now we'll take this further with two powerful structures for emphasis in formal English.

1. Inversions for Emphasis

In formal or written English, we can invert the subject and auxiliary to add dramatic emphasis. This is common in essays, speeches, and academic writing.

Standard: If it had not been for Darwin, our understanding of life would be completely different.

Inverted: Had it not been for Darwin, our understanding of life would be completely different.

Standard: If Anne Frank had not kept her diary, we would have lost a vital personal perspective on the Holocaust.

Inverted: Had Anne Frank not kept her diary, we would have lost a vital personal perspective on the Holocaust.

Standard: If Orwell had not written 1984, we would not have the word "Orwellian."

Inverted: Had Orwell not written 1984, we would not have the word "Orwellian."

The pattern: Remove "if" → move the auxiliary (had/were/should) to the front.

2. Cleft Sentences for Emphasis

Cleft sentences split a simple sentence into two parts to highlight one particular element. They are extremely useful in argumentation.

Neutral: Darwin's book changed biology forever.

What-cleft: What changed biology forever was Darwin's book.

It-cleft: It was Darwin's book that changed biology forever.

Neutral: The personal voice made Anne Frank's diary so powerful.

What-cleft: What made Anne Frank's diary so powerful was the personal voice.

It-cleft: It was the personal voice that made Anne Frank's diary so powerful.

Use "what...was" to emphasise an action or quality. Use "it was...that" to emphasise a specific noun.
