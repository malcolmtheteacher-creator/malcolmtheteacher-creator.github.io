Strategic Networking

Building real connections that become lasting business relationships

The Problem

Most business cards from trade fairs end up in the bin.

You spend three days at a fair, you have great conversations, you exchange contact details with 50 people... and then nothing happens. Why?

The disconnect

What most people do: Collect cards. Move to the next person. Repeat. The goal becomes the card, not the relationship.

What actually works

Strategic networking: Listen more than you speak. Find genuine points of connection. Make clear commitments. Follow up with intention. The goal is trust, not collection.
Why do you think collecting many cards isn't the same as building real relationships? What's the difference between transactional contact and a genuine connection?

The GIVE Framework

Strategic networking isn't about selling. It's about exchange. Use GIVE to build real connections:

G – Genuine Interest

Start by being interested, not interesting. Ask real questions about their challenges, goals, and context.

Examples:
  • "What challenges are you facing in supply chain right now?"
  • "How is your team handling the shift to remote work?"
  • "What's your biggest priority this quarter?"

I – Identify Shared Goals

Listen for common ground. Where do your interests overlap? What problem do you both solve?

Examples:
  • "We had a similar situation with cost management last year."
  • "That's interesting—we're both focused on sustainability."
  • "I think we might be serving similar clients."

V – Value Exchange

Offer something specific and useful. Not a sales pitch—something they actually need: information, an introduction, a resource, a perspective.

Examples:
  • "I think there could be some synergy between your operations team and our consultants."
  • "Can I send you the report we did on this? It might help your planning."
  • "I know someone in logistics you should talk to—shall I introduce you?"

E – Exit with Action

Don't let the conversation end in vagueness. Make a specific, concrete commitment.

Examples:
  • "Can I send you that case study next week? Let's aim for Tuesday."
  • "I'll set up a call with my lead engineer—shall we do Thursday or Friday?"
  • "I'm going to research your company and follow up with three specific ideas. Expect an email from me by Wednesday."
Practise each part. Choose a person you might meet at a fair and create: 1) A genuine question for G, 2) A point of connection for I, 3) Something you could offer for V, 4) A specific next step for E.

Transactional vs Relationship Networking

The difference between collecting contacts and building relationships:

Transactional

Goal: Collect as many cards as possible.

Question: "So what do you do?"

Time: 3 minutes, then move on.

Follow-up: Generic email to 50 people.

Result: Ignored or deleted.

Relationship

Goal: Find mutual benefit and stay in touch.

Question: "What challenges are you solving right now?"

Time: 10 minutes of real conversation.

Follow-up: Specific email with something relevant.

Result: They reply. Coffee call. Partnership.

The Situation

You're on the airport shuttle with someone from a logistics company. They're checking their email, but you have 20 minutes together.

Your opening: "Long fair?"

Genuine conversation about their fair experience. 3 minutes.

03:00

Your aim: Listen more than you speak. Find out what brings them to this fair. What's their biggest challenge right now? How might you stay connected without being pushy?

Scenario 3: Standing Next to a Speaker

The Situation

A keynote speaker is standing alone at the coffee station. You found their talk valuable. You want to say hello without being creepy or over-eager.

Your opening: Start naturally.

Build a genuine connection. 3 minutes.

03:00

Your aim: Be specific about what resonated from their talk. Ask a real question. Don't ask them to work with you—ask what they're working on.

LinkedIn Connection Message

After the fair, you connect with someone on LinkedIn. This is your chance to stand out from the 100 generic "Nice to meet you" messages.

The Formula

  • Specific reference: Mention something you discussed or observed about them
  • Personal note: Why this connection matters to you
  • Value hint: Something you might be able to help with (or just genuine interest)
  • Light next step: Keep it optional—don't demand anything

Examples

Example 1: "Hi Alex, great chat about your logistics expansion into eastern Europe. I was impressed by your timeline—that takes real planning. I've worked with three companies on similar expansions and would be happy to share what we learned about local compliance. No pressure, but worth a coffee if you're open to it. All the best, Sam"
Example 2: "Hi Jordan, lovely to meet you at the sustainability panel. Your point about carbon tracking resonated—we're wrestling with the same problem. Would be great to stay connected and see if there are ideas worth sharing. Here's to the work ahead, Chris"

Reflection

Remember the Five Frameworks

You've now learned the core frameworks for trade fairs. Let's check your memory:

Lesson 1: How do you sell in 30 seconds?

HOOK-SOLVE-PROVE — Grab attention with a hook, show the problem you solve, prove it works with evidence or credibility.

Lesson 2: How do you explain the real value?

FAB (Feature → Advantage → Benefit) — Move from what you have (feature) to what it does (advantage) to why they care (benefit).

Lesson 3: How do you negotiate?

PREP (Position → Reason → Evidence → Proposal) — State your position, explain why, back it up with evidence, then propose a way forward.

Lesson 4 (today): How do you build real relationships?

GIVE (Genuine interest → Identify shared goals → Value exchange → Exit with action) — Listen, find common ground, offer something real, commit to next steps.

Now you can...

I can ask genuine questions that show interest rather than just pitching. I can listen for shared challenges and find points of connection. I can offer something valuable without making it transactional. I understand that relationships, not card collections, lead to real business.
