Crisis Management & Difficult Conversations

Turning conflict into an opportunity to show leadership

You're in the Hot Seat

Scenario: It's 2pm on day two of the trade fair. Your company's biggest client — worth €500k in annual business — walks up to your booth. Their face is tight. You can feel something's wrong.

They say: "I need to talk to you. Privately. We're seriously considering switching to your competitor. Your team has been unresponsive. We gave you a contract extension last month and nothing's changed."

The pressure:

  • Your booth is busy. Everyone's watching.
  • You didn't know about this issue (someone dropped the ball).
  • If you lose this client, it affects your fair. It affects your job.
  • Your instinct is to defend, to explain, to fix it right now.
  • But they're angry. If you talk too much, it gets worse.

What Makes This Different from a Normal Negotiation

In a regular conversation, there's space to explore. In a crisis, emotions are high. Trust is broken (or cracking). Time is compressed.

Most people fail here because they:

  • Jump to explaining (sounds like excusing)
  • Ask questions (when the person needs to be heard first)
  • Make promises (before they understand what went wrong)
  • Get defensive (which hardens their position)
Think of a time you felt unheard or let down in business. What would have changed your mind about leaving? What did you actually need from the other person?

In this lesson, you'll learn LEAD — a framework for crisis moments that turns anger into an opening to rebuild trust.

The LEAD Framework

LEAD is for high-stakes, emotionally charged conversations. It prioritises understanding over defending. Trust over winning.

Listen
Empathise
Assess
Decide

1. LISTEN FULLY (First 2 minutes)

Goal: Hear them completely. Don't interrupt. Don't defend.

This is hard because listening activates your anxiety. Your brain is already planning your response. Quiet that. Just listen.

  • Your face matters: Stay calm, stay open.
  • Your body matters: Don't cross your arms, don't look at your phone.
  • Use "I want to make sure I fully understand your concern..." — buys time, signals respect.
  • Ask ONE clarifying question: "Help me understand — what specific moments made you feel unheard?"
  • Repeat back what you heard: "So you're saying..."

2. EMPATHISE AUTHENTICALLY (45 seconds)

Goal: Show you get it. Not to agree, but to acknowledge.

This is NOT "I understand how you feel" (generic, dismissive). This is specific recognition of their legitimate concern.

  • "I can see this is important to you..." — names the emotion
  • "You have every right to be frustrated. We let you down." — takes responsibility
  • "If I were in your position, I'd be looking at other options too." — normalises their choice
  • Avoid: "I know how you feel" or "Don't be upset" (minimises their emotion)

3. ASSESS OPTIONS (2–3 minutes)

Goal: Make the problem concrete. What actually needs to happen?

  • "Let me be transparent about what happened..." — honesty is disarming
  • Name the specific breakdown: "Our account team didn't do X, and that's on us."
  • Distinguish between systemic (we broke process) and personal (a person didn't care): "This isn't about commitment — it's about systems. We're fixing it."
  • Ask: "What would it take for you to feel like we're serious about this?"

4. DECIDE & COMMUNICATE CLEARLY (2 minutes)

Goal: Propose a path forward. Make it specific, make it real.

  • "Here's what I propose we do..." — ownership language, not committee-speak
  • "I take full responsibility for..." — personal accountability is rare and powerful
  • Propose next steps, not promises: "I'm going to call your manager within 48 hours with a recovery plan. Here's what I'll need from you..."
  • Set expectations: "I can't undo what happened, but I can control what happens next."

Language That Works in Crisis

Instead of: "You're overreacting." / Say: "I hear that this feels urgent to you."

Instead of: "We have lots of clients." / Say: "You matter to us, and I want to prove that."

Instead of: "It wasn't my fault." / Say: "I take responsibility for making sure this gets fixed."

Instead of: "Let me explain..." / Say: "Let me listen first. Go ahead."

Three Real Crisis Scenarios

Here are three crises that actually happen at trade fairs. For each, I'll show you what happens when people DON'T use LEAD, and what changes when they do.

Crisis 1: Product Recall Announced During the Fair

What happens: Mid-afternoon, you get an email from your head office. "Possible defect in batch XR-400. Recall being issued within 2 hours. Be prepared for questions."

Your booth is packed. People are asking for that exact product.

Weak Response:

What goes wrong

"We're aware there's a potential issue. We're handling it with our main office. It's probably nothing. These things happen. You can still place an order if you want."

  • Minimises the issue ("probably nothing")
  • Hides behind "main office" (no ownership)
  • Tries to keep selling (tone-deaf)
  • Leaves people uncertain about what to do

Strong Response (Using LEAD):

"I appreciate you asking. I'm going to be direct: We've identified a potential issue with batch XR-400. We're issuing a recall as a precaution — that's the responsible thing to do. If you've ordered that batch, I'm personally overseeing a replacement with our next production run, free of charge, within 30 days. I can email you the full details right now. What I want you to know: We stand behind our product, and we're not hiding this."

  • Owns the issue immediately
  • Explains action (not excuse)
  • Gives specific remedy
  • Rebuilds trust by being transparent

Crisis 2: Key Team Member Doesn't Show Up

What happens: It's 9am on day one. Your technical specialist doesn't show up. No message. Their phone goes to voicemail. You have 10 product demos booked for the day.

Weak Response:

What goes wrong

"I'm so sorry, our tech person isn't here. I'm not sure what happened. Let me try to help, but I'm not as technical as he is. Maybe we can reschedule?"

  • Sounds panicked and unprepared
  • Blames absence ("what happened?")
  • Tells people you CAN'T help them (wrong message)
  • Turns it into their problem (rescheduling)

Strong Response (Using LEAD):

"I want to be upfront: our specialist isn't here today due to an emergency. Rather than apologise for that, what I want to do is give YOU the best experience anyway. I know the product inside-out. I've done 50 implementations. Let me show you something — not everything the specialist could, but enough to decide if this is worth exploring. Then, if you want, I'll personally set up a follow-up with the full technical team next week. Sound fair?"

  • Honest about the problem
  • Reframes from absence to capability
  • Takes ownership of the experience
  • Offers a path forward (next week meeting)

Crisis 3: Competitor Makes False Claims About Your Product

What happens: You overhear the booth next to you telling a visitor: "Their product has major security issues. We fixed ours, but they're still using the old vulnerable system."

It's not true. But the visitor now has doubt.

Weak Response:

What goes wrong

You walk over angrily: "That's completely false. Your product isn't better than ours. You're lying to get sales."

  • Makes YOU look defensive and angry
  • Creates a public confrontation
  • Visitor thinks both companies are unprofessional
  • The false claim now SOUNDS true (because you're so reactive)

Strong Response (Using LEAD):

You calmly catch up to the visitor: "I overheard something that I want to address directly. Our product uses X security protocol. We published our security audit last month — I can show it to you. The claim you just heard isn't accurate, and here's why. I'd never criticise a competitor publicly. I'd rather you decide based on facts. Want to see our audit?"

  • Calm, not defensive
  • Facts, not emotion
  • Gives evidence (the audit)
  • Takes the high road (no competitor attack)

Three High-Pressure Situations

You're going to practice three different crisis conversations. Each gets progressively harder. You have 3 minutes per scenario to respond authentically using LEAD.

Situation 1: Angry Client at Your Booth

Setup: A long-time client arrives furious. They say: "I spent €80,000 with you last year. My implementation failed. Your team never called to check in. I found out by accident that another customer with the same setup had the same issue — you KNEW about it. Why didn't anyone warn me?"

Your goal: Listen fully. Don't interrupt to explain. Show you understand why they're angry.

Situation 2: Journalist Asking Hostile Questions

Setup: A journalist from a trade publication approaches your booth. She says: "I'm writing a piece on quality issues in your industry. I've heard from three companies that your product has reliability problems. Can you respond to that?"

Your goal: Stay calm. Don't attack the journalist. Address the concern with facts and transparency.

Situation 2: 3 minutes
3:00
Respond to the journalist. Remember: She's looking for a story. If you're defensive, that's the story. If you're transparent and calm, that changes everything.

Situation 3: Partnership Going Wrong Publicly

Setup: A partner company you co-exhibited with is publicly criticising you on social media. They posted: "Disappointed by [Your Company]. They promised support and delivered nothing. We're ending the partnership." It's getting attention. Several fair visitors have seen it.

Your goal: Move from public criticism to private resolution. Stay professional. Don't escalate on social media.

Situation 3: 3 minutes
3:00
You spot the partner walking the fair. You approach them. How do you de-escalate? Remember: LEAD starts with listening. Don't defend the partnership yet — find out what actually went wrong.

Self-Assessment After Each Roleplay

  • Did you interrupt, or did you listen?
  • Did your face and body show openness, or did you tense up?
  • Did you empathise, or did you jump to defending?
  • Was your close an invitation to solve it together, or a dismissal?

Internal Briefing: Preparing Your Team

Imagine your team is going to the fair with you. You need to brief them on how to handle difficult conversations when they happen (and they will).

Your Written Task:

Write a briefing note (200–300 words) for your team covering:

  1. What counts as a crisis: A complaint? A competitor attack? A partnership breakdown? All of these.
  2. Your LEAD protocol: What's our process? Listen first, empathise second, then solve.
  3. Red flags: What should someone escalate immediately? (E.g., product safety, legal threat, major client leaving)
  4. Language that works: Give 3–4 phrases your team should use when things get tense.
  5. Your own conflict style: How do YOU naturally respond when under pressure? What's your instinct? (Defend? Placate? Withdraw?) How will you manage that?

Understanding Your Conflict Style

Everyone has a default when things get stressful. Knowing yours is crucial.

Avoider: You go quiet, step back, hope it blows over.

Under pressure at the fair: You might disappear when a client is angry, leaving your team exposed.

LEAD adjustment: Force yourself to LISTEN first. That's harder for you, but it's the antidote.

Defender: You jump straight to explaining, justifying, defending your position.

Under pressure at the fair: You interrupt the angry client with "But actually..." — which makes it worse.

LEAD adjustment: Silence is your enemy. Bite your tongue. Ask one clarifying question. Listen.

Placater: You try to smooth it over, make everyone happy, say yes to everything.

Under pressure at the fair: You promise things you can't deliver: "I'll fix it today," "You're definitely our priority."

LEAD adjustment: Be honest about what you CAN'T do. "I can't undo what happened, but here's what I can control..."

Which conflict style is closest to you? What happens to you under pressure? And how will you catch yourself at the fair and use LEAD instead?
