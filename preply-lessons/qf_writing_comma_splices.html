Stop the Splice

B2 Level · Writing & Grammar · Fixing Run-On Sentences

The Comma Splice Problem

A comma splice happens when you join two complete sentences with only a comma. Each side of the comma could stand alone as its own sentence — but instead they have been glued together incorrectly.

This is one of the most common errors in IELTS and academic writing. It makes your writing look rushed and uncontrolled, even when your ideas are good.

Can You Feel It?

Read these sentences out loud. Where do you naturally want to stop and take a breath?

1. "The government invested heavily in public transport, the number of car users dropped significantly over the following decade."

COMMA SPLICE. Two complete ideas joined by just a comma. You can feel the natural break after "transport" — that is where one sentence ends and another begins.

2. "Although the economy recovered, unemployment remained high in rural areas."

CORRECT. "Although the economy recovered" is not a complete sentence on its own — it depends on the second part. The comma is fine here.

3. "Remote working has become standard practice, many employees now refuse to return to the office full-time."

COMMA SPLICE. Both halves are independent sentences. The comma is doing a job it cannot do alone.

4. "The research was funded by the university, which had recently received a large government grant."

CORRECT. The second part starts with "which" — a relative clause that adds information about the university. It is not an independent sentence.
The Test: Cover the comma. Can both sides stand alone as complete sentences with a subject and a verb? If yes, you probably have a comma splice. If one side depends on the other (starts with although, because, which, who, that, etc.), the comma is fine.

Four Ways to Fix a Comma Splice

Every comma splice has the same problem: two independent clauses joined by only a comma. Here are four tools to fix it. Each one changes the tone slightly.

Fix 1
Full Stop Split into two sentences. Simple, clear, and always safe.

"The cost of living rose. Many families struggled to pay rent."
Fix 2
Semicolon Use a semicolon when the two ideas are closely connected and you want to show that relationship.

"The cost of living rose; many families struggled to pay rent."
Fix 3
Conjunction Add a coordinating conjunction (and, but, so, yet, or) after the comma.

"The cost of living rose, so many families struggled to pay rent."
Fix 4
Subordination Make one clause dependent using because, although, when, since, as, etc.

"Because the cost of living rose, many families struggled to pay rent."

Which Fix, When?

Match each situation to the best fix.

The two ideas are related but you want to keep them short and punchy.

Fix 1: Full stop. Two short sentences create impact and clarity. This works especially well in conclusions.

One idea is the cause and the other is the effect.

Fix 4: Subordination. Use because, since, as to show the cause-effect relationship clearly. This adds sophistication to your writing.

The two ideas contrast with each other.

Fix 3: Conjunction. Use but or yet after the comma to signal the contrast. You could also use Fix 4 with although or while.

The ideas are two sides of the same coin — closely related and equally weighted.

Fix 2: Semicolon. A semicolon says "these ideas belong together" without making one more important than the other. Use sparingly in IELTS — one or two per essay maximum.
IELTS Tip: Examiners notice comma splices because they directly affect your Grammatical Range & Accuracy score (Band 7 requires "frequent error-free sentences"). Fixing comma splices is one of the fastest ways to improve from Band 5–6 to Band 7.

Fix These Sentences

Each sentence below contains a comma splice. Say your correction out loud first, then check the suggested fixes.

1. "Technology has transformed education, students can now access lectures from anywhere in the world."
Full stop: Technology has transformed education. Students can now access lectures from anywhere in the world.
Conjunction: Technology has transformed education, and students can now access lectures from anywhere in the world.
Subordination: Since technology has transformed education, students can now access lectures from anywhere in the world.
2. "The experiment failed to produce significant results, the researchers decided to redesign their methodology from scratch."
Full stop: The experiment failed to produce significant results. The researchers decided to redesign their methodology from scratch.
Conjunction: The experiment failed to produce significant results, so the researchers decided to redesign their methodology from scratch.
Semicolon: The experiment failed to produce significant results; the researchers decided to redesign their methodology from scratch.
3. "Many people believe that social media improves communication, others argue that it actually makes us more isolated and less connected to the people around us."
Conjunction: Many people believe that social media improves communication, but others argue that it actually makes us more isolated.
Subordination: While many people believe that social media improves communication, others argue that it actually makes us more isolated.
Semicolon: Many people believe that social media improves communication; others argue that it actually makes us more isolated.
4. "Public libraries are struggling to survive, funding has been cut dramatically over the past decade, many communities have lost their only access to free books and internet."
Mixed fix: Public libraries are struggling to survive. Funding has been cut dramatically over the past decade, and many communities have lost their only access to free books and internet.
Subordination: Because funding has been cut dramatically over the past decade, public libraries are struggling to survive, and many communities have lost their only access to free books and internet.

Notice: When a sentence has TWO comma splices, you often need a combination of fixes.

5. "The city invested millions in cycling infrastructure, the number of cyclists tripled within three years, air pollution levels fell to their lowest point in decades."
Mixed fix: The city invested millions in cycling infrastructure. As a result, the number of cyclists tripled within three years, and air pollution levels fell to their lowest point in decades.
Subordination: After the city invested millions in cycling infrastructure, the number of cyclists tripled within three years and air pollution levels fell to their lowest point in decades.
Key Insight: Long sentences are not bad. Comma splices are bad. You can write beautiful long sentences using conjunctions (and, but, so), subordinators (because, although, when), and relative clauses (which, who, that). The comma splice happens when you skip these connectors entirely.

Self-Check

Before you finish, read your paragraph and check each comma:

The Comma Test: For every comma in your paragraph, ask: "Can both sides of this comma stand alone as complete sentences?" If yes — you need a full stop, a conjunction, a semicolon, or a subordinator. If one side depends on the other, the comma is doing its job correctly.

How many of the four fixes did you use in your paragraph?

Check your writing:

• Did you use at least one full stop to separate ideas? (Fix 1)

• Did you use a semicolon between closely related ideas? (Fix 2 — optional but impressive)

• Did you use and, but, so, or yet to join clauses? (Fix 3)

• Did you use because, although, while, since, or when? (Fix 4)

A strong IELTS paragraph typically uses a mix of all four. If you only used commas and full stops, try revising one sentence with a subordinator.
