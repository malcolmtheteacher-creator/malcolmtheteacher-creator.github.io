Follow-Up Communication

72 hours to turn conversations into contracts

The Race Against Time

The fair ends on Friday. By Tuesday, your chances of a real conversation drop by 70%. By next Monday, most leads are dead.

Why? Because everyone forgets quickly, and your competitors are emailing too.

The 72-hour window

Your follow-up within 48 hours separates you from 80% of the people at that fair. Not because your message is brilliant, but because it's there when they're still thinking about the event.

The math: If you meet 20 people and follow up with only 3 of them properly, you'll likely convert 1 into a real lead. If you send generic follow-ups to all 20, you'll convert 0. But if you send specific, thoughtful follow-ups to 20 people within 48 hours? You'll convert 3-5.

What Actually Works

The five things every follow-up needs
  • Specificity: Reference something from your conversation, not a generic template
  • Speed: Within 48 hours, ideally within 24
  • Value: Offer something useful: information, an introduction, a resource, a next step
  • Personalization: Your voice, not "Dear Sir or Madam"
  • Clarity: One clear next step (call, email, meeting), not vague "let's stay in touch"

The STAR Follow-Up Framework

STAR guides every follow-up communication: email, phone call, message. Use it consistently.

S – Specific Reference

Remind them of something unique from your conversation. This proves you were actually paying attention and valued the interaction.

Examples:
  • "I enjoyed our chat about your expansion into Southeast Asia—especially your point about local logistics partners."
  • "You mentioned you're implementing a new ERP system this quarter. That's exactly what we specialise in."
  • "The problem you described with supply chain visibility is something we solved for three clients in your industry."

T – Thank

Be genuine. Thank them for their time, their openness, their questions. This builds goodwill.

Examples:
  • "I really appreciated your time—it's not often I meet someone so knowledgeable about the market."
  • "Thanks for being so open about your challenges. That kind of transparency helps us understand how to actually help."
  • "Your questions were sharp. They made me think differently about how we present our solution."

A – Action

Offer something concrete and useful. Not a sales pitch. Something they actually need.

Examples:
  • "I'm sending you the case study I mentioned—it's from a company similar to yours. Check pages 4-6 for the ROI breakdown."
  • "I want to introduce you to our logistics partner in Bangkok. I think you two should talk."
  • "Here's a link to our webinar on ERP implementation. Our CTO explains the common pitfalls in your industry."

R – Remind of Value

Close with a gentle reminder of why staying connected makes sense. Not "We should do business," but "There's something worth exploring here."

Examples:
  • "If the ERP project moves forward, I'd love to be a sounding board. We've learned a lot in this space."
  • "I think there's real synergy between what you're building and what we do. Let's explore it."
  • "You're solving a problem we see across the sector. I think we can help each other win."

Good vs Bad Follow-Up Emails

See the difference between follow-ups that get deleted and ones that start conversations:

The Generic Copy-Paste

Subject: "Great Meeting You"

"Hi there, thanks for visiting our stand. It was great meeting you at the fair. Our products might be perfect for your company. Let's set up a call. Looking forward to hearing from you!"

Problem: Could be sent to anyone. Shows you weren't listening. Vague next step. Gets deleted.

The Specific, Valuable Follow-Up

Subject: "Re: Your ERP timeline—here's what we learned"

"Hi Sarah, enjoyed our conversation about your Q3 ERP implementation. You mentioned concerns about data migration—I've attached a short guide on managing that phase. It's what we did for TekkCorp (similar scale to you) and it saved them 30% of implementation time. Also, I know our integration specialist well—happy to introduce if it helps. Let's connect next week?"

Why it works: Specific reference, genuine value, clear next step, personal touch.

Email Template (Use This Structure)

Subject: [Specific reference from conversation—their challenge or goal]

Hi [Name],

[SPECIFIC]: [One unique thing from your conversation that shows you listened]

[THANK]: [Genuine thanks for their time or insight]

[ACTION]: [One useful thing you're offering—resource, intro, perspective]

[REMINDER]: [Why it makes sense to stay connected]

Let's [specific next step: coffee call / email in two weeks / quick call Thursday].

[Your name]

Five Email Rules

  • Keep it short: 4-5 paragraphs max. They're busy.
  • One clear ask: Don't ask them to schedule a meeting AND fill out a survey AND review materials.
  • Make it easy to say yes: "Can we do Thursday or Friday?" beats "let's set up a time."
  • Proofread: Typos make you look careless in a business context.
  • Send fast: Within 24 hours ideally. Definitely within 48.

Phone & Video Follow-Up

Sometimes email isn't enough. A phone call or video chat shows you're serious about the relationship. Practice these scenarios:

Scenario 1: The Phone Call

Situation: You're calling someone you met at the fair. They probably don't remember your name yet. You have 2 minutes before they get busy.

Structure: Warm greeting → remind them how you met → one specific thing → ask for a better time to chat.

02:00

Scenario 2: The Voicemail

Situation: You called. It went to voicemail. You have 20 seconds to leave a message that makes them want to call back.

Structure: Your name and company → how you met → one specific reason you're calling → ask them to call back OR offer an alternative (email, text).

00:45

Scenario 3: The Video Call Pitch

Situation: They agreed to a video call. You have 15 minutes. First 2 minutes are about reconnection, not pitching.

Structure: Warm greeting → ask them how things are going since the fair → explore their challenges → only then talk about how you might help.

Lesson 1: How do you sell your company in 30 seconds?

HOOK-SOLVE-PROVE — Capture attention with a hook, show the problem you solve, provide proof of your ability to solve it.

Lesson 2: How do you explain real value?

FAB (Feature → Advantage → Benefit) — Start with what you have, move to what it does, end with why they care.

Lesson 3: How do you negotiate win-win deals?

PREP (Position → Reason → Evidence → Proposal) — State your position clearly, explain your reasoning, back it up with evidence, propose a way forward.

Lesson 4: How do you build real business relationships?

GIVE (Genuine interest → Identify shared goals → Value exchange → Exit with action) — Show interest, find common ground, offer real value, make clear commitments.

Lesson 5: How do you handle tough objections?

FEEL-FELT-FOUND — Validate their feeling, show they're not alone, share what others discovered when they moved forward.

Lesson 6: How do you follow up to turn conversations into contracts?

STAR (Specific reference → Thank → Action → Remind of value) — Reference something unique from your chat, thank them genuinely, offer something useful, remind them why it matters.
