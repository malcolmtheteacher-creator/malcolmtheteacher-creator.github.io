Cross-Cultural Communication

When your handshake, eye contact, and sense of humour might be "wrong"

The Invisible Barriers

You're at an international trade fair. You walk up, extend your right hand for a handshake, make strong eye contact, smile warmly, and say: "Great to meet you!"

The person across from you steps back slightly, looks down, and takes your hand briefly.

You just made three mistakes. And you didn't know.

Here's what happened:

  • In their culture, eye contact with a stranger shows disrespect
  • The warmth you showed came across as presumptuous — you haven't earned relationship yet
  • Your directness feels aggressive to them

The problem: these aren't rules written down anywhere. They're cultural defaults — so embedded in how people think that they don't even notice them. Until someone violates them.

At an international fair, you'll meet people with radically different defaults:

  • Some believe business is built on relationships first, deals second
  • Some believe deals are deals — relationships follow (or don't matter)
  • Some will expect you to be very direct about pricing and terms
  • Some will see that directness as rude and will negotiate indirectly instead
  • Some will make decisions based on hierarchy (what the boss wants)
  • Some will expect consensus from their team
  • Some will see your humour as professional and clever
  • Some will see it as unprofessional
Think about your own culture. What communication style feels "normal" to you? Direct or indirect? Relationship-first or deal-first? Now: what would feel strange or rude if someone did it?

The goal today isn't to learn 200 different cultures. It's to develop cultural awareness — so you can adapt in real time when you sense a mismatch.

Key Cultural Dimensions

These dimensions don't define cultures — they're awareness tools. Use them to read the room.

1. Direct vs. Indirect Communication

Direct: "This won't work. Here's why." Clear, explicit, saves time.

Indirect: "That's an interesting idea. In my experience, we've seen some challenges with similar approaches..." Relationship-preserving, but slower.

Watch for: Does the person tell you "no" directly, or do they say "yes" but mean maybe? Are they comfortable with blunt feedback?

2. Relationship-First vs. Deal-First

Relationship-First: "Let's get to know each other. Trust matters. Only then do we talk business."

Deal-First: "Let's talk about what you need, what I can offer, and whether there's a fit. Relationship follows."

Watch for: Do they want to exchange business cards immediately, or do they ask about your family first? Is small talk expected or time-wasting?

3. Hierarchy Sensitivity

High Hierarchy: "We need to involve the decision-maker. Titles matter. You address the most senior person."

Low Hierarchy: "Anyone on the team can decide. We make decisions together. Titles are just for organising."

Watch for: Do they defer to someone else before answering? Do they insist you speak to "the right person"? Do they seem uncomfortable making decisions without approval?

4. Attitude Toward Time

Clock Time: "2pm means 2pm. Be on time. Respect people's schedules. Efficiency matters."

Event Time: "2pm is approximate. The conversation ends when it's done. Rushing feels disrespectful."

Watch for: Are they checking their watch? Did they arrive exactly on time or 15 minutes late? Are they rushing the conversation?

5. Physical Space & Touch

Close Space: "Handshakes, shoulder touches, standing near. Touch shows warmth."

Far Space: "Distance shows respect. Touch is only for close friends."

Watch for: Do they step closer or back away? Do they reciprocate a handshake or just touch your fingers? Do they make physical contact?

6. Eye Contact

Direct Eye Contact: "I'm being honest. I'm confident. I'm engaged."

Avoiding Eye Contact: "I'm showing respect. I'm not challenging your authority."

Watch for: Are they looking at you directly or at the ground? Is their gaze steady or do they break contact frequently?

Pick three of these dimensions. For each one, what's YOUR natural style? (Example: "I'm relationship-first, direct communication, low hierarchy, clock-time." Now: how would someone with opposite preferences experience meeting you?

When Things Go Wrong (And How to Fix It)

Here are three real-world misunderstandings at trade fairs. Each one happened because of clashing cultural defaults. Watch how the person recovers.

Scenario 1: The Direct Feedback Disaster

You: "Your product spec has a problem. The API architecture won't scale to your customer base."

Them: [Long silence. They look uncomfortable.]

You: [Trying to help] "Don't worry, it's fixable. Here's what you need to do..."

Them: "I'll have to speak with my team about this."

[You've just told them they were wrong, in front of others, and given them a solution without consulting their team. In their culture, this is deeply disrespectful.]

How to recover

Step back. Reframe: "I apologise — I came across too direct there. What I meant to say is: I see a potential scalability consideration. Your team probably has thought about this already. How are you approaching growth beyond, say, 10,000 concurrent users?"

This does three things: (1) You acknowledge the mistake, (2) You give them credit for expertise, (3) You invite them to solve it together.

Scenario 2: The Relationship Impatience

Them: [After 5 minutes] "Tell me about your family. Where are you from? Do you have children?"

You: [Eager to move forward] "I'm happy to chat, but I know your time is valuable. Should we focus on your needs first?"

Them: [Polite but noticeably cooler] "Of course. Yes, let's talk business."

[You just told them that you don't care about them as a person, only about getting a deal. That signals you're not trustworthy.]

How to recover

Lean in. Invest time in the relationship: "I apologise. Actually, I'd love to know more. Where are you based? Have you been to this fair before?"

Then — even if you're impatient — give them time. Answer their questions about yourself. Ask about their team. Show genuine interest. The business conversation will come. And when it does, they'll be much more open because you've shown them you see them as a person, not just a customer.

Scenario 3: The Hierarchy Miss

You: [To the younger team member] "So what does your company need in a logistics partner?"

Younger person: [Looking uncomfortable] "That's a question for our operations director."

You: "Sure, but you work on this day-to-day. What's your perspective?"

Younger person: [Even more uncomfortable] "I'd prefer to wait for the director."

[You've tried to elevate them by giving them voice, but in their culture, that's putting them in an impossible position. Decisions come from the top.]

How to recover

Read the signal. Adapt: "Got it. Let's make sure we have the right conversation. When can we all three talk? Is there a time that works for your team?"

You're respecting hierarchy by asking to include the decision-maker. You're making it easy for the younger person. And you're showing respect for how their organisation works.

Communicating with Different Styles

You're at a booth. I'll play four different visitors with very different communication styles. Each one is a potential buyer. Your job: adapt in real time.

Scenario 1: Very Direct, Deal-First Communicator

Them: "Hi. What do you do and how much does it cost?"

This person wants efficiency. No small talk. They respect directness. Give them clarity and numbers. The conversation should be 2–3 minutes unless they ask follow-up questions.

You have a 3-minute timer. How would you pitch to this person? Go.

Scenario 3: Hierarchy-Conscious, Formal Communicator

Them: [In formal tone] "Good afternoon. I am the procurement manager for our region. Do you have materials for senior leadership to review?"

This person expects protocol. Use titles. Be professional. Ask if their director or CEO needs to be involved. Don't try to be their buddy. Professional distance is respectful here.

I'm a procurement manager. I expect formality and clarity. Show me you understand our structure and decision process. Go.

Scenario 4: High-Context, Indirect, Relationship-Focused Communicator

Them: [Smiling] "I've heard interesting things about your company. Tell me — what made you choose this industry?"

This person is reading you as much as your product. They want to understand your values and motivations. Be genuine. Tell them a real story. Don't over-pitch. Let them ask questions. 10–15 minutes of genuine conversation.

I'm asking about your motivations. I want to understand your story. Answer genuinely, then ask me about mine. Build a real conversation. Go.

Prepare Your Team: A Cultural Awareness Checklist

Before you go to an international fair, your team needs a simple guide. Not a 50-page handbook — just awareness reminders. Here's a template.

Your Task:

Create a one-page checklist for your team. Use these prompts:

  1. Our defaults: How do WE communicate naturally? (Direct/indirect? Relationship/deal-first? Hierarchy-aware?)
  2. Likely differences: Which dimensions might clash with visitors at this fair?
  3. Watch for: What signals tell us someone has a different style? (E.g., "If they don't make eye contact, don't assume they're uninterested...")
  4. If we sense a mismatch: What's our first move? (Pause. Ask. Adapt.)
  5. Questions we'll ask: What are three open questions we can ask to understand their communication style before we pitch?
Draft this checklist now. Speak it aloud. (Yes, actually speak it to me — does it sound like something a real team would use?) Then tell me: What's the ONE cultural dimension where YOUR team is most likely to make a mistake?

Pro Tip:

The best checklist is SHORT. One page. Your team won't read a handbook. But they'll remember: "If something feels off, pause and ask."
