Handling Objections

Turn tough questions into your competitive advantage

The Challenge

72 hours into a three-day fair. A visitor at your stand says: "I've heard your delivery times are unreliable."

Your heart rate increases. In that moment, do you:

  • Get defensive? "That's not true, actually..."
  • Panic and drop your price?
  • Change the subject?
What actually works: Stay calm. Listen. Understand why they believe this. Turn it into a conversation about their real concern. That objection might be your best opportunity.

Why objections are opportunities

An objection means they're interested. They're engaging. They're testing you. If you handle it well, you separate yourself from every competitor who got defensive or fell silent.

Think of a tough question or objection you've heard about your industry, company, or product. How did you react? What would have worked better?

The FEEL-FELT-FOUND Framework

This classic framework works because it validates the other person before it educates them. It says: "I get it. You're not alone. And there's something you might not know."

F – FEEL: Validate Their Feeling

Start by acknowledging their concern as real. Don't dismiss it. Not "You're wrong," but "That's fair."

Opening phrases:
  • "That's a fair point."
  • "I appreciate you raising that."
  • "I understand how you'd think that."
  • "That's a concern we hear."

E – FELT: Show They're Not Alone

Share that others have felt the same way. This normalises the objection and builds connection.

Opening phrases:
  • "Many of our prospects have felt the same way initially."
  • "We worked with a firm just like yours that had the same concern."
  • "That was actually my first thought too when we started exploring this."
  • "Others in your industry have mentioned that."

F – FOUND: Share What They Found

Now you explain what happened when they moved forward anyway. Use specific evidence: data, a case, a testimonial, or your own experience.

Opening phrases:
  • "What we found was..."
  • "Here's what changed their mind..."
  • "When we looked at the numbers, it turned out..."
  • "In our experience, the real issue was something different."

Putting It Together

Full example — Price objection:

"Your prices are too high."

FEEL: "I understand—budget is always a real factor."
FELT: "We hear this from a lot of prospects initially, especially when they're comparing us to the cheaper options."
FOUND: "What they found was that the cheaper option ended up costing more in the long run because of poor support and rework. When we mapped out the total cost over two years, our approach actually saved them 30%."

Then ask: "Would it help if I showed you that comparison?"

Bridging Phrases

After you validate, felt, and found, bridge back to forward momentum:

Examples:
  • "Would it help if I showed you...?"
  • "Can I ask you something—if we could address that, would it change things?"
  • "Here's what I'd suggest..."
  • "Let me walk you through how we'd handle that differently."

Five Types of Objections

Most objections fit into a few categories. Knowing the type helps you respond strategically.

1. Price Objection

They say: "It's too expensive." / "I can find the same thing cheaper."

What they really mean: "I don't see the value yet." / "I don't trust that it's worth the cost."

Strategy: Never just discount. Instead, show ROI, compare total cost of ownership, or highlight unique value others miss. FEEL-FELT-FOUND their budget concern, then show what the investment brings back.

2. Quality/Trust Objection

They say: "I've heard you had problems with..." / "Are you reliable?"

What they really mean: "I need to trust you before I commit."

Strategy: Don't get defensive. Share specific examples of how you've improved, testimonials, certifications, or track record. Invite them to verify themselves.

3. Timing Objection

They say: "Not right now." / "We're not ready yet." / "Maybe next year."

What they really mean: "I'm not convinced this is urgent." Or: "I need to sell this internally first."

Strategy: Explore the real timeline. Are they actually not ready, or are they not convinced? Help them think through urgency. Offer a light commitment (a call in 3 months, a follow-up email).

4. Competing Solution Objection

They say: "We're already working with [competitor]." / "Someone else does this for us."

What they really mean: "Why should I switch or add another vendor?"

Strategy: Don't criticise the competitor. Ask what they like about their current solution and where the gaps are. Position yourself as complementary or superior in specific ways.

5. Authority/Veto Objection

They say: "I'd have to check with my manager." / "Our procurement won't approve this."

What they really mean: "I'm interested, but I need to justify this to someone else."

Strategy: Help them make the case. Offer materials for their internal conversation, ask about the approval criteria, or offer to speak with the decision-maker yourself.

Reflection

Recall the Four Frameworks So Far

You've now learned the frameworks for pitching, benefits, negotiating, networking, and objections. Test yourself:

When someone says "Tell me about your company," what framework do you use?

HOOK-SOLVE-PROVE — Hook their attention, show the problem you solve, prove it with credibility.

How do you explain why your features matter?

FAB (Feature → Advantage → Benefit) — Move from what you have, to what it does, to why they care.

When negotiating, what's your structure?

PREP (Position → Reason → Evidence → Proposal) — State your position, explain why, provide evidence, propose a way forward.

How do you build real business relationships?

GIVE (Genuine interest → Identify shared goals → Value exchange → Exit with action) — Listen, find common ground, offer something real, commit to next steps.

When someone raises an objection, how do you respond?

FEEL-FELT-FOUND — Validate their feeling, show they're not alone, share what others found when they moved forward.
