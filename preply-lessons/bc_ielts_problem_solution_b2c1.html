IELTS Writing Task 2

Problem & Solution Essays  ·  B2–C1

What is a Problem & Solution essay?

You are given a social, environmental, or global issue and asked to identify its causes or problems and then propose solutions. The examiner wants to see developed ideas — not just a list.

Recognising the question type

Typical prompt patterns:

▸ "What are the causes of [problem] and what solutions can be suggested?"

▸ "What problems does [development] create, and how could these be resolved?"

▸ "Discuss the problems associated with [issue] and suggest possible solutions."

The four-paragraph structure

1
Introduction
Paraphrase the prompt. Mention that you will discuss both problems and solutions. Do not list your points here.
2
Problems Paragraph
Identify two main problems. For each one, explain why it is a problem — give a result or consequence. Don't just name it.
3
Solutions Paragraph
Propose two specific solutions — one for each problem if possible. Explain the likely result of each solution.
4
Conclusion
Briefly restate the main problems and the proposed solutions. Express an outlook — cautiously optimistic or realistic.

Think about this:

Why does IELTS ask for solutions as well as problems? What does this task reveal about a candidate's range of ideas and language?

Is it possible to give a good answer if you only describe the problem and then say "the government should fix it"? Why/why not?

Building a solutions paragraph — sentence by sentence

A high-scoring solutions paragraph follows a clear internal logic. Here is the standard six-sentence pattern for a two-solution paragraph.

1
Topic sentence
Signal the paragraph's focus. "There are several steps that could be taken to address these issues."
2
Solution 1
Introduce your first proposed measure. Be specific about who does it and what it involves.
3
Result of solution 1
Explain the positive outcome of this measure. Use result language: This would lead to… / As a result… / This could significantly reduce…
4
Linking sentence
Transition smoothly to the second solution. "A further measure would be to…" / "Additionally, …"
5
Solution 2
Introduce your second proposed measure.
6
Result of solution 2
Explain the positive outcome. This closes the paragraph with a forward-looking note.

Model paragraph — can you identify each step?

Read this solutions paragraph and match each sentence to the six steps above.

[A] There are a number of measures that could be introduced to address the problem of urban congestion and pollution. [B] Firstly, city governments could invest in affordable, reliable public transport — expanding bus and metro networks so that private car use becomes unnecessary for most journeys. [C] This would directly reduce the volume of vehicles on the road, lowering both traffic levels and harmful emissions. [D] A further approach would be to introduce congestion charging in city centres, making it financially costly to drive into the busiest areas. [E] This policy has already proven effective in cities such as London and Stockholm. [F] If adopted more widely, it could serve as a significant disincentive for unnecessary private car journeys.

[A] Topic sentence → [B] Solution 1 → [C] Result of solution 1 → [D] Linking sentence + Solution 2 → [E&F] Result / development of solution 2

Language for proposing solutions

Using a range of structures makes your essay sound academic and avoids repetition. Click each card to see the pattern and an example.

could + base verb Tentative recommendation.
Governments could invest in renewable energy infrastructure.
should be required to Stronger obligation.
Companies should be required to reduce their carbon emissions annually.
One effective solution would be to Introduces a specific measure formally.
One effective solution would be to introduce stricter regulations on industrial waste.
This would lead to Explains the result of a proposed measure.
This would lead to a significant reduction in air pollution in major cities.
A further measure would be to Transitions to a second solution.
A further measure would be to raise public awareness through education campaigns.
If … were to …, this could Conditional structure for hypothetical solutions.
If governments were to ban single-use plastics, this could dramatically cut ocean pollution.

Sentence building practice

Use the prompt and language cue to write a full sentence. Check your answer when ready.

Topic: air pollution in cities. Solution: subsidise electric vehicles for low-income households.
→ Use: One effective solution would be to
One effective solution would be to subsidise the purchase of electric vehicles for low-income households, making cleaner transport accessible to all.
Topic: youth unemployment. Solution: businesses partner with schools to offer apprenticeship programmes.
→ Use: should be required to
Businesses should be required to partner with local schools and colleges to offer structured apprenticeship programmes for young people.
Topic: urban congestion. Solution: introduce congestion charging. Result: reduce car journeys into city centres.
→ Use: This would lead to
Congestion charges could be introduced in city centres. This would lead to a significant reduction in the number of car journeys into the most heavily congested areas.

Discuss:

Why is it better to use could or would be to rather than must or will when proposing solutions in an IELTS essay?

Brainstorm and plan your essay

Read the prompt and complete the planning stages below before you write.

📝 The prompt:

Many cities around the world are experiencing serious traffic congestion and high levels of air pollution. What are the main problems caused by this, and what solutions could be suggested?

Step 1 — Identify two problems (with their consequences):

Problem 1: Health impact — long-term exposure to vehicle emissions causes respiratory diseases such as asthma and lung cancer, particularly in children and elderly residents.

Problem 2: Economic cost — traffic congestion wastes millions of working hours annually as commuters sit in gridlock, reducing business productivity and GDP.

Step 2 — Propose a matching solution for each problem:

Solution 1 (for health): Governments could introduce low-emission zones, banning diesel vehicles from city centres. This would reduce harmful pollutants near densely populated areas.

Solution 2 (for congestion): Investment in fast, affordable public transport — such as underground rail or bus rapid transit — would encourage commuters to leave their cars at home.
