How to Play

Welcome to Grammar Detective! Your mission is to find and identify grammar errors hidden in natural-sounding paragraphs. This isn't about spotting obvious mistakes — these errors are the kind that native speakers sometimes make, or that learners often struggle with.

You'll examine 4 case files, each containing 2–3 errors. Read carefully, spot the mistakes, and then reveal the corrections to understand the grammar rules at play.

Practice Case: Error-Free Paragraph

Read this paragraph. It contains NO errors. Get a feel for correct grammar:

"My sister has lived in London for five years. She works at a big company in the city centre. She told me that she really enjoys her job, although the commute is sometimes tiring."

✓ This paragraph is correct. All verb tenses, prepositions, and structures are accurate.

Practice Case: One Error

Can you spot the error here?

"She said me that she would call later."

The Error:

said me → told me
Rule: "Say" does not take an indirect object. Use "tell" instead. Correct: "She told me that..." or "She said that she would..."

Case 1: The New Job
"I've been working at my new company since three months now, and I really enjoy it. My boss suggested me to take a training course next month, which sounds like a good idea. If I would pass the exam at the end, I might get a promotion."

Errors Found:

Error 1:
since three months → for three months
Rule: Use "for" with durations (three months, two years). Use "since" with points in time (since January, since 2020).
Category: Tenses & Aspect
Error 2:
suggested me to take → suggested that I take or suggested I take
Rule: After "suggest", use "that + subject + base verb". NOT "suggest + person + to".
Category: Verb Patterns (Gerunds & Infinitives)
Error 3:
If I would pass → If I passed
Rule: Second conditional uses past simple after "if", NOT "would". "Would" only goes in the result clause.
Category: Conditionals
Case 2: Last Weekend
"Last weekend, we went to a restaurant that was recommended by a friend. The food was absolutely delicious, despite of the long wait for our table. I had never been eating there before, so everything felt special. The waiter, that was very friendly, even gave us a free dessert."

Errors Found:

Error 1:
despite of the long wait → despite the long wait
Rule: "Despite" is NEVER followed by "of". Use "despite + noun" or "in spite of + noun".
Category: Conjunctions & Connectors
Error 2:
had never been eating → had never eaten
Rule: Use past perfect simple (had + past participle) for completed experiences before a past moment. Past perfect continuous would be "had been eating" but the meaning here requires simple.
Category: Tenses & Aspect
Error 3:
The waiter, that was → The waiter, who was
Rule: In non-defining relative clauses (with commas), use "who" for people, NEVER "that".
Category: Relative Clauses
Case 3: Travel Plans
"My friend asked me where did I want to go on holiday this year. I told her that I'm thinking about visiting Japan, because I've always been interested about Japanese culture. She said it was a great idea and offered to help me plan the trip."

Errors Found:

Error 1:
where did I want → where I wanted
Rule: In reported/indirect questions, use statement word order (subject before verb), NOT question word order. Also applies tense backshift: want → wanted.
Category: Reported Speech
Error 2:
interested about → interested in
Rule: The correct preposition after "interested" is always "in".
Category: Prepositions
Case 4: Health and Fitness
"I've been going to the gym since January, and I definitely feel more healthy now. I used to be quite lazy, but a friend persuaded me to join. In the beginning, I didn't used to enjoy the early mornings, but now I'm completely used to waking up at six. The trainer says if I keep going, I'll see even better results by summer."

Errors Found:

Error 1:
more healthy → healthier
Rule: One/two-syllable adjectives ending in -y use -ier form (healthy → healthier). Don't use "more" with these.
Category: Adjectives & Adverbs
Error 2:
didn't used to → didn't use to
Rule: In the negative past, it's "didn't use to" (base form after "did"). The -d is on "did", not on "use".
Category: Tenses & Aspect

Complete Error Summary

All 10 errors from the case files are listed below, organized by grammar category. Each error includes the correction and the rule it violates.

📌 Tenses & Aspect (4 errors)

Case 1, Error 1: Duration vs. Point in Time
since three months → for three months
Use "for" with durations; use "since" with points in time.
Case 2, Error 2: Past Perfect Simple vs. Continuous
had never been eating → had never eaten
Past perfect simple for completed experiences; don't use continuous unless duration is emphasized.
Case 4, Error 2: Negative Past Habit
didn't used to → didn't use to
Base form goes after "did" in negatives. The -d stays on "did".

📌 Conditionals (1 error)

Case 1, Error 3: Second Conditional
If I would pass → If I passed
Second conditional: past simple in the if-clause, would + base in the result clause.

📌 Verb Patterns & Infinitives (1 error)

Case 1, Error 2: Suggest + Structure
suggested me to take → suggested that I take or suggested I take
"Suggest" takes "that + base verb", not "to". Never use suggest + person + to.

📌 Conjunctions & Connectors (1 error)

Case 2, Error 1: Despite vs. Despite of
despite of the long wait → despite the long wait
"Despite" is never followed by "of". Use "despite + noun" or "in spite of + noun".

📌 Relative Clauses (1 error)

Case 2, Error 3: Non-Defining Relative Clause
The waiter, that was → The waiter, who was
In non-defining relative clauses (with commas), use "who" for people, never "that".

📌 Reported Speech (1 error)

Case 3, Error 1: Indirect Questions
where did I want → where I wanted
Indirect questions use statement word order. Tense also shifts back: want → wanted.

📌 Prepositions (1 error)

Case 3, Error 2: Interested + Preposition
interested about → interested in
The preposition after "interested" is always "in".

📌 Adjectives & Adverbs (1 error)

Case 4, Error 1: -y Adjective Comparatives
more healthy → healthier
One/two-syllable adjectives ending in -y use -ier (happy → happier, healthy → healthier). Don't use "more".
