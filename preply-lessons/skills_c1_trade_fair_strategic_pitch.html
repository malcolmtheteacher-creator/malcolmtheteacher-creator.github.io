Why you matter in a room full of 200 competitors

What Makes You Unforgettable?

Imagine you're at a trade fair with 200 companies in your space. Every booth looks professional. Everyone has a product. Everyone can explain what they do.

So why should anyone remember YOU instead of the other 199?

Think about this:

  • Most visitors will see 15–20 booths, not 200
  • They'll remember perhaps 3 companies
  • Memory isn't about who explained the most — it's about who made them feel something

What are the unforgettable pitches?

Not the ones that say: "We provide X services in Y industry."

The unforgettable ones:

  • Start with a question that matters to THEM
  • Show you understand their world — their pain, their pressure
  • Tell a story (a real one, with conflict and resolution)
  • Give them one concrete, surprising fact that changes how they think
  • Invite them to imagine a different future — together with you
When you go to a trade fair, what makes YOU remember a company? What did they do differently? Why did they stick with you?

In this lesson, you'll learn to craft pitches that win because they're not just heard — they're felt.

The AIDA Framework

AIDA is a classic persuasion model. At a trade fair, you need all four to move someone from "interesting" to "let's talk further."

Attention
Interest
Desire
Action

1. ATTENTION

Goal: Stop them. Make them pause.

Without attention, everything else fails. In 3 seconds, they decide: "Is this for me?"

  • "What if I told you..." — creates curiosity
  • "Most companies in your space make the same mistake..." — signals understanding
  • A striking visual or stat — memory anchor

2. INTEREST

Goal: Show them this is about THEM, not you.

  • Name their challenge specifically: "Your supply chain is vulnerable to..."
  • Show you've done research: "Companies in your sector lose on average..."
  • Ask a question: "How much does it cost you every time...?"

3. DESIRE

Goal: Show them what's possible. Paint the picture.

  • "Here's what that means in practice..." — make it concrete
  • "I'd love to show you exactly how this works..." — invitation language
  • Give evidence: case study, data, testimonial (from a company like theirs)

4. ACTION

Goal: Next step is clear and easy.

  • "Let's grab 15 minutes tomorrow — when suits you better, morning or afternoon?"
  • "Can I send you a one-pager on how this works for companies like yours?"
  • "What would you need to see to move forward?"

Analysing Real Pitches

Let's look at three pitches for the same product. Same company. Same fair. Different results.

Pitch A: The Weak One

"Hi, we're TechFlow Solutions. We provide cloud-based software for logistics companies. We've been in business for 8 years and we work with over 150 clients across Europe. Our system is very flexible and we have 24/7 customer support. Would you like to know more?"
Why this doesn't work
  • Generic: "Cloud-based software" could be anyone. No specific problem named.
  • About them, not you: "We've been in business 8 years" — I don't care about your timeline, I care about MY problem.
  • No story: Just facts. Facts fade. Stories stick.
  • Weak call-to-action: "Would you like to know more?" is a yes/no question. Visitors say "No thanks" and move on.

Pitch B: The Stronger One

"Most logistics companies lose 12–18% of their margin to inefficiencies they never see — late shipments, double-bookings, manual handoffs. One of our clients lost €400k last year before they fixed it. We help companies like yours find those invisible costs and eliminate them. In fact, most of our clients recover their software costs within 6 months. Can I show you how we spotted this in your sector?"
Why this works better
  • Named problem: "Inefficiencies," "double-bookings" — specific language about their world.
  • Proof: "One of our clients lost €400k" — makes it real and urgent.
  • What's in it for them: "Recover costs in 6 months" — clear benefit.
  • Invitation: "Can I show you..." is an invitation, not a gate. Feels collaborative.

Pitch C: The Excellent One

"The real question isn't whether your logistics system is efficient — it's whether you'd recognise inefficiency if it walked right past you. Most companies can't. They lose money they don't even know about. One logistics director we work with discovered his team was manually re-keying shipment data that their previous software was supposed to handle automatically. It was costing £8,000 a month in labour, and he'd never seen it until we helped him look. That's what we do: we make the invisible visible. Can I spend 10 minutes helping you spot what might be hidden in your operation?"
Why this works best
  • Rhetorical question: "The real question isn't X, it's Y" — shifts their thinking.
  • Story with emotion: The director who didn't see his own waste — he's relatable.
  • Specific detail: "£8,000 a month in labour" — money. That's memorable.
  • Clear value proposition: "We make the invisible visible" — one sentence that says everything.
  • Collaborative close: "Help you spot" (not "sell you") + bounded ask (10 minutes, not vague).
Which of these three pitches would stick with you? Why? What did the excellent pitch do that the weak one didn't?

Same Product, Different Audiences

Here's the secret: the SAME solution needs DIFFERENT pitches depending on who's listening. You're pitching the same software to four different roles. Each one cares about something different.

Scenario: You work for a fintech company

Your product: a regulatory compliance platform that helps banks spot risky transactions in real time.

Pitch 1: The CFO (Cares about cost, risk, ROI)

"Non-compliance costs banks an average of €2m per incident. We've helped our clients reduce compliance failures by 87% and cut investigation time from 8 hours to 12 minutes. The ROI is typically 300% in year one. What's your current compliance cost per transaction?"

Pitch 2: The Technical Director (Cares about architecture, integration, scalability)

"Our platform integrates with existing core banking systems via REST API, handles up to 10 million transactions per day, and returns risk scores in under 200 milliseconds. We use machine learning models that adapt to your institution's risk profile. How many daily transactions are we talking about?"

Pitch 3: The End User (Front-line analyst — cares about ease of use, time, frustration)

"Right now, checking a transaction takes you how long? Our clients go from suspicious transaction to clear or escalate decision in under 90 seconds. The alerts learn your patterns, so you spend less time on false positives. Want to see how it works?"

Pitch 4: The Journalist (Cares about story, impact, differentiation)

"Banks are fighting a losing battle against financial crime. Traditional methods are reactive — they see the crime after it happens. We've built a system that's predictive. We can show you how one bank caught a €5m fraud 72 hours before it would have gone undetected. That's the story."

Craft Your One-Page Pitch

Now you'll create a pitch document. Think of a product or service you know (real or imagined). Structure it using the AIDA framework, and use at least one rhetorical device (question, metaphor, story).

Your Task:

Write a one-page pitch (300–400 words) that:

  1. Opens with a rhetorical question or surprising statement (ATTENTION)
  2. Names a specific problem your audience faces (INTEREST)
  3. Gives a concrete example or case study showing the solution in action (DESIRE)
  4. Closes with a clear, bounded next step (ACTION)
Draft your pitch now. Speak it aloud as you write it — does it sound natural? Does it feel like YOU? Once you've written it, read it back to me, and I'll ask you three questions: (1) What's the ONE thing they should remember? (2) Why did you choose that rhetorical device? (3) What's your goal with that close?

Pro Tip:

Forget the product for a moment. What does your buyer's world look like RIGHT NOW? What keeps them awake? Start there. Everything else follows.
