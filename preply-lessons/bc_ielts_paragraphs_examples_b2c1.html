The five elements of a strong body paragraph

A high-scoring IELTS paragraph isn't just about having ideas — it's about structuring them so the examiner can follow your argument clearly and completely.

1
Main idea
The topic sentence — states the central point of the paragraph clearly. Everything else develops this idea.
2
Opposing view (optional)
Acknowledge the counterargument before dismissing it. This shows balance and critical thinking. "While some argue that... it is clear that..."
3
Explanation
Explain why your main idea is true or important. Don't just restate it in different words.
4
Example
Give a specific, concrete example to support the explanation. This is evidence that your claim is grounded in reality.
5
Conclusion sentence
Close the paragraph by linking back to your position or summarising the significance of the point.

Handling opposing views within a paragraph

In a well-developed paragraph, you can acknowledge what the other side might argue — and then show why your point is stronger. This is called a concession-rebuttal pattern.

The concession-rebuttal pattern:

While it is true that [opposing point], it is clear that [your main point is stronger] because [reason/evidence].

Model sentence:

"While it is true that a high salary provides financial security, research consistently shows that employees who feel a strong sense of purpose in their work report higher levels of long-term satisfaction than those motivated primarily by pay."

While it is true that Concedes the opposing point before overturning it.
While it is true that exams create stress, they remain a reliable measure of academic ability.
Although some argue that Introduces the opposing view before dismissing it.
Although some argue that salary is the primary motivator, job satisfaction studies suggest otherwise.
Admittedly, … however Acknowledges validity, then pivots.
Admittedly, flexible hours can complicate team coordination. However, the productivity gains are substantial.
It could be argued that… yet Hedged concession — softer tone.
It could be argued that job security is a luxury for many workers. Yet evidence shows it strongly affects mental health outcomes.
Main argument: Career growth opportunities are the most important job factor. Opposing view: Some people prioritise salary above all else.
→ Write a concession-rebuttal sentence using "While it is true that"
While it is true that salary is an important consideration for many workers, opportunities for career growth and professional development are arguably more valuable in the long term, as they provide both financial rewards and a sense of ongoing achievement.

How to introduce examples in IELTS writing

Examples add credibility and depth to your argument. But IELTS rewards how you introduce them — not just that you use them. Click each card to see the phrase and an example.

For example, / For instance, Most common. Simple and clear.
For example, in countries such as Finland, teachers are required to complete a master's degree, resulting in consistently high educational outcomes.
A clear example of this is Introduces a specific case with emphasis.
A clear example of this is the success of Singapore's education system, which combines rigour with strong teacher development.
This is illustrated by Connects the example directly to the point being made.
This is illustrated by the case of Japan, where long working hours have been linked to a public health crisis.
Evidence suggests that Introduces research or data-based support. Does not require a specific statistic.
Evidence suggests that students who learn music at an early age perform better in mathematics and language tasks.
As [name] once said, "..." Introduces a quote for emphasis. Use sparingly — only if you know the quote accurately.
As Nelson Mandela once said, "Education is the most powerful weapon which you can use to change the world."
Studies have shown that Formal evidence marker. Does not require citing a specific study — use it for widely accepted findings.
Studies have shown that children who read for pleasure consistently outperform those who do not in both literacy and general knowledge.

Quote practice

These famous education quotes can be used in IELTS Writing Task 2 essays. Read each one, then think of a claim you could use it to support.

"Education is the most powerful weapon which you can use to change the world." — Nelson Mandela

This quote supports arguments about the importance of investing in public education, the role of education in reducing inequality, or the long-term social benefits of universal schooling. Use it after making a claim about education's societal impact.

"An investment in knowledge always pays the best interest." — Benjamin Franklin

This supports arguments about lifelong learning, the economic value of higher education, or why governments should fund education generously. It works well as a closing sentence to reinforce an argument about education spending.

"If you give a man a fish, you feed him for a day. If you teach a man to fish, you feed him for a lifetime." — Ancient proverb (often attributed to Lao Tzu or Confucius)

This supports arguments that education and skills training are more valuable than direct financial aid, that long-term solutions to poverty require education rather than charity, or that vocational training should be prioritised.

Error correction

Each of these sentences contains a common IELTS mistake. Identify the error, then check the corrected version.

1. "Education is very important because it helps people to get a good job and also it is useful for society."
Problems: Too vague ("very important", "good job", "useful for society") and informal repetition ("also it is"). Missing a specific explanation or example.

Improved: "Education plays a pivotal role in economic development, equipping individuals with the skills required by a modern workforce and enabling societies to reduce unemployment and increase productivity."
2. "In conclusion, I think that the government should invest more in education because it is the key of the future."
Problems: "the key of the future" is not natural English (should be "the key to"); "I think" is too informal for a conclusion; the conclusion doesn't summarise the essay's arguments.

Improved: "In conclusion, it is clear that sustained government investment in education represents the most effective long-term strategy for social and economic progress, as argued throughout this essay."
3. "For example, Finland is a country where students do not have a lot of homework but they get very high scores on international tests, it shows that less pressure can lead to better results."
Problem: This is a comma splice — two independent clauses joined only by a comma. The second clause needs to be a separate sentence or joined correctly.

Improved: "For example, Finland demonstrates that students can achieve outstanding results in international assessments without excessive homework or examination pressure. This suggests that well-being and academic performance are not mutually exclusive."
4. "Some peoples argue that online learning is as better as traditional classroom education."
Problems: "peoples" — the noun "people" is already plural; "as better as" is incorrect — the comparison should be "as good as" (not "as better").

Improved: "Some people argue that online learning is as effective as traditional classroom education, particularly when students have access to quality digital resources and reliable internet connectivity."
