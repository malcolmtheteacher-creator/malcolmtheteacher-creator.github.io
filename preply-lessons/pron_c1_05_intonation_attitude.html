English intonation boils down to three core shapes. The pitch movement happens on the last stressed syllable of the phrase ‚Äî this is called the nucleus or tonic syllable.

üí° Why the fall-rise matters: Many languages don't use this pattern. If you default to flat or falling intonation when you mean "maybe" or "but...", you can accidentally sound blunt, rude, or overly direct. British speakers use the fall-rise constantly ‚Äî in polite refusals, hedging, giving opinions, and softening disagreement.
üéØ Quick summary: Fall = I'm sure, I'm done. Rise = I'm asking, or I'm not done. Fall-rise = there's more to say, I'm being careful, or "but..."

üéØ Tonic stress ‚Äî where you put the pitch

The pitch pattern lands on the most important word ‚Äî the word that carries the new or contrasting information. Moving the tonic stress changes the meaning completely.
