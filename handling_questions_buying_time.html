Handling Questions and Buying Time

What to say when you don't know the answer

The Hook: When You Don't Know the Answer

You're in a meeting. A client asks you a question.
You don't know the answer.

What do you do?

This happens to everyone. CEOs, managers, experts - nobody knows everything. The difference between good professionals and great professionals? How they handle it.

73%

of business professionals say they have felt "panic" when asked a question they couldn't answer in a meeting.

Source: LinkedIn Workplace Survey, 2023

The Problem: Silence

When we don't know an answer, our brain freezes. In our own language, we can say "Hmm, let me think about that." But in English? Many people just... stop talking. This looks bad. It makes you seem unprepared.

4 sec

Four seconds of silence feels like one minute in a business meeting. After 4 seconds of silence, people start to feel uncomfortable.

Source: University of Cambridge Communication Studies

Marco's Story - Milan, Italy

Marco is a sales director for a food packaging company. He was in a video call with German buyers. They asked: "What is your carbon footprint per unit?" Marco didn't know. He said nothing for 5 seconds. Then he said "I... um... I don't..." The Germans looked at each other. Marco felt terrible. He lost the deal.

Later, Marco learned: "If I had just said 'That's a great question - I can get you that number today' - they would have respected me. Silence made me look unprepared. Having a phrase ready makes you look professional."

Anna's Story - Warsaw, Poland

Anna runs a small pet food company (like you!). She was meeting with a UK supermarket buyer. The buyer asked: "What's your monthly production capacity?" Anna wasn't 100% sure of the exact number. Instead of guessing wrong, she said: "Good question. I want to give you the exact number, so let me check and email you this afternoon." The buyer smiled and said "That's fine - I appreciate accuracy."

Anna got the contract. The buyer later told her: "I liked that you didn't make up a number. I can trust you."

The Secret: It's Not What You Know

Good professionals know one important thing: it's OK not to know everything. What matters is HOW you respond.

89%

of managers say they respect employees MORE when they honestly say "I don't know, but I'll find out" instead of guessing or staying silent.

Source: European Management Review, 2022

In this lesson, you'll learn 5 simple phrases that help you:

  • Buy time to think
  • Ask for clarification
  • Admit you don't know (professionally)
  • Promise to find the answer

Phrases A: Buy Time and Ask for Help

Click each card to learn the phrase, then practice with "Now You Try."

1 "That's a good question. Let me think..."

Click to learn more

When to use it

When you need a few seconds to think. This phrase buys you time AND makes the other person feel good (you called their question "good").

How to say it

That's a GOOD question. (pause) Let me THINK...

Say "good" with emphasis. Then pause. This gives you 3-4 seconds to think!

Example

Client: "How long until you can deliver to our warehouse in Germany?"

You: "That's a good question. Let me think... I believe it's about 5-7 working days, but I can confirm that."

NOW YOU TRY

Situation: A supplier asks you: "What percentage of your sales come from online?"

You're not 100% sure. Use the phrase to buy time, then give an approximate answer.

Start with:

That's a good question. Let me think...

Then say something like:

I think it's about... If I remember correctly... I believe it's around...

2 "Sorry, can you say that again?"

Click to learn more

When to use it

When you didn't hear clearly, or when you need more time. Asking someone to repeat gives you extra seconds to think. It's also completely normal - even native speakers do this!

How to say it

Sorry, can you say that again?

Say "sorry" quickly and politely. Stress "again" at the end.

Variations

"Sorry, could you repeat that?" (slightly more formal)

"Sorry, I didn't catch that." (very natural)

Example

Partner: "What's your position on the new EU packaging regulations?"

You: "Sorry, can you say that again? The connection wasn't clear."

NOW YOU TRY

Situation: You're on a video call. A client asks a long question about shipping costs. You heard the question, but you need time to think. Ask them to repeat it.

Choose one:

Sorry, can you say that again? Sorry, could you repeat that? Sorry, I didn't catch that.

Optional - add a reason:

The audio cut out for a moment. I want to make sure I understand correctly.

3 "What do you mean by [X]?"

Click to learn more

When to use it

When someone uses a word or idea you don't understand. This is SMART - it shows you want to give a good answer. It also buys you thinking time.

How to say it

What do you MEAN by [word/phrase]?

Stress "mean" slightly. Put the confusing word at the end.

Example

Buyer: "What's your sustainability roadmap?"

You: "What do you mean by 'roadmap'? Do you mean our plans for the next year, or longer term?"

NOW YOU TRY

Situation: A business partner says: "We need to discuss your go-to-market strategy for Poland."

You're not sure what "go-to-market strategy" means exactly. Ask for clarification.

Start with:

What do you mean by "go-to-market strategy"?

Then you can add:

Do you mean how we plan to sell there? Are you asking about our sales plan? Do you mean our marketing approach?

Phrases B: When You Really Don't Know

These phrases are for when you truly don't have the answer. Being honest is always better than guessing!

4 "I'm not sure, but I can check and tell you later."

Click to learn more

When to use it

When you don't know the answer but you CAN find it. This shows you're honest AND professional - you'll follow up.

How to say it

I'm not sure, but I can CHECK and tell you LATER.

Be specific about WHEN you'll tell them if possible.

Variations

"I'm not sure, but I can find out and email you today."

"I'm not sure, but I can check with my team and get back to you."

"I don't have that number with me, but I can send it this afternoon."

Example

Client: "What's the protein percentage in your premium dog food?"

You: "I'm not sure of the exact number, but I can check and email you this afternoon. Is that OK?"

NOW YOU TRY

Situation: A potential buyer asks: "How many retail stores in France currently stock your products?"

You don't know the exact number. Respond professionally.

Start with:

I'm not sure of the exact number... I don't have that figure with me...

Then promise to follow up:

...but I can check and email you today. ...but I can find out and get back to you this week. ...but I can ask my sales team and let you know.

5 "Actually, I don't know. Let me find out."

Click to learn more

When to use it

When you completely don't know something. This is the most honest response. "Actually" shows you're being genuine. Studies show people TRUST this more than a bad guess!

How to say it

Actually, I don't know. Let me find out.

Say "actually" to show you're being honest. Keep your voice confident - not knowing is OK!

Why this works

Many people try to hide when they don't know something. But business partners can usually tell when you're guessing. Being honest builds trust. Saying "Let me find out" shows you're professional.

Example

Investor: "What's your customer acquisition cost?"

You: "Actually, I don't know that number exactly. Let me find out from my finance team and include it in my follow-up email."

NOW YOU TRY

Situation: A distributor asks: "What percentage of pet food sales in Europe are online versus in stores?"

This is about the whole market, not your company. You really don't know. Respond honestly.

Be honest:

Actually, I don't know. To be honest, I'm not sure about the whole market.

Then offer to help:

Let me find out. I can research that and send you what I find. I can ask our industry association.
